The following list of primes k · 2n + 1 is an extension and a current update of Table 5 in Hans Riesel's book Prime Numbers and Computer Methods for Factorization [ Riesel94]. Further extensions to higher multipliers k are given for  300 < k < 600,  600 < k < 900,  and  900 < k < 1200.  A listing like:

k   n1, n2, . . ., nh [Lim]

indicates that the list of primes for n = n1, n2, . . ., nh is complete for all n < Lim. In a listing like:

k   n1, n2, . . ., nh [Lim] nh+1, nh+2, . . .

further known primes are added without knowledge of a higher limit possibly reached through systematic search. So the partial list of primes for n = nh+1, nh+2, . . . may or may not be complete up to the highest prime known. Just for these primes the individual prover code is attached in each case. For successive primes found by the same discoverer, the code is given only once.

The larger of the primes are included in Chris Caldwell's list of the largest known primes. If you are interested in the primes by their overall ranking rather than by values of k, or to know who first discovered them, you should search the database for primes of the form k · 2n + 1. To access information on primes that are no more on the main list, the option All verified primes might be helpful. Finally, an excerpt from the current prime list for a rectangular range k1 < k < k2, n1 ≤ n ≤ n2 can easily be obtained.

An exponent n set in bold in the list below represents a prime which is a factor of some Fermat number Fm with m ≤ n − 2. Presently, 368 such factors are known. For more specific information, see the complete factoring status of Fermat numbers.
